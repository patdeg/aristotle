## Main post (copy-paste this)

My twelve-year-old is people-smart, not math-smart. He comes home from class looking a little lost. Good teacher, thirty-two other kids, the math is somewhere in the room but it isn't in him.

I'd just finished writing Chapter 5 of a book about education. The factory model is over, every kid can have an AI tutor. Then I watched him struggle with fractions and the chapter started looking at me.

So one weekend I built it.

The tool reads his Canvas grades and writes him a weekly two-page Socratic worksheet in the voice of Aristotle. Every scenario is set in his favorite thing: ancient military logistics. He plays a Roman legion quartermaster. A fort commander holding the walls against barbarians. The math is the same sixth-grade ratios and inequalities. But now the quartermaster needs to ration amphorae, and my kid needs to do the math to find out.

Two moments my wife stopped being skeptical:

(1) I said "three exercises is enough." He insisted on doing all five.

(2) The next day, tired after a long hike, I was ready to skip it. He came to me. "Dad, can we do the second section?"

A twelve-year-old, unprompted, asking to do more math.

Bloom's 2-sigma problem (1984): a kid with a one-on-one tutor outperforms 98% of peers. Unsolvable for 40 years because we had 50M kids and 0 spare Aristotles. LLMs solved it last Tuesday.

It's open source, MIT-licensed, free. The book is on Amazon, the audiobook is on Spotify.
