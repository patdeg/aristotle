## Recommended version (~1,200 chars, fits the LinkedIn preview)

My twelve-year-old is people-smart, not math-smart. He comes home from class looking a little lost. Good teacher, thirty-two other kids, the math is somewhere in the room but it is not in him.

I'd just finished writing Chapter 5 of a book about education — the factory model is over, every kid can have an AI tutor, etc. Then I watched him struggle with fractions and the chapter started looking at me.

So one weekend I built it.

The tool reads his grades from Canvas, pulls the topics he missed, and writes him a two-page Socratic worksheet in the voice of Aristotle — every scenario set in his favorite thing, which is ancient military logistics. He plays a Roman legion quartermaster. A fort commander holding the walls against barbarians. The math is the same sixth-grade ratios and inequalities. But now the quartermaster needs to ration amphorae, and my kid needs to do the math to find out.

Two moments my wife stopped being skeptical:

(1) I said "three exercises is enough." He insisted on doing all five.

(2) Next day, after a long hike, I was tired and ready to skip it. He came to me. *"Dad, can we do the second section?"*

A twelve-year-old, unprompted, asking to do more math.

Bloom proved in 1984 that a one-on-one tutor lifts a kid two standard deviations. Call it the 2-sigma problem — unsolvable for 40 years because we had 50M kids and 0 spare Aristotles. LLMs solved that problem last Tuesday.

On GitHub, MIT-licensed, free forever. ~$100/year to run (domain + email). Two hours to set up.

## Tone notes

- Lead with the concrete scene (twelve-year-old, lost in class). LinkedIn rewards specificity.
- Mention "I wrote the chapter" once, not twice — credibility, not self-promotion.
- The two eyebrow moments are the entire post. They're the thing a stranger will remember.
- End on the concrete call: $100/year, 2 hours, GitHub. Don't make the reader guess what to do.
- Tag the book with the Chapter 5 link — the link does the work of the pitch.
- No emoji in the body. Only at the footer, where they read as signposts.
