# The idea behind Aristotle

> *"With Large Language Models, every child on Earth can have a personal Aristotle."*
> Patrick Deglon, *The Unscarcity Blueprint*, [Chapter 5: The Education of a Citizen](https://unscarcity.ai/a/chapter5)

This repo is a working artifact of one specific idea from that chapter. It's worth a few minutes to read why the software is shaped the way it is.

---

## The problem the factory was solving

In 1843, Horace Mann sailed to Prussia and brought back a school system that was designed, in Prussia, on purpose, to produce obedient soldiers and compliant bureaucrats. Bells, rows of desks, silence, standardized curriculum, quality-control tests. It was brilliant at what it was built for: processing farm kids into factory workers.

That system educated you. It educated me. It's still the one we send our kids to.

> *"We've trained an entire civilization to act like machines, and now the actual machines have arrived to do the job for real."*
>, [Education: Factory vs. Citizen](https://unscarcity.ai/a/education-factory-vs-citizen)

The factory model produced compliant rule-followers. That's exactly what machines do better. We're training kids to be inferior robots.

---

## What the chapter asks for instead

Chapter 5 names three things the factory doesn't teach, and AI makes possible to teach to every kid, one kid at a time:

### Pillar 1, Systems Thinking

Don't teach fragmented subjects. Teach the connections between them. *"A citizen needs to understand the machine they live inside. Not just the parts, the architecture."* When Aristotle writes a worksheet scenario, it deliberately crosses domains: a ratio problem embedded in a historical supply chain, a graph problem embedded in a game economy, a science problem that pulls on math the child already knows.

### Pillar 2, Humanities (narrative ethics)

*"What matters is the practice of examining stories to understand what values they embody and whether those values are worth living by."* Every Aristotle worksheet opens with a scenario the child has to judge, not just compute on. The Socratic questions before the math are the point; the math is the tool the kid reaches for when the story demands it.

### Pillar 3, Agency

*"School shouldn't be a place where you consume knowledge; it should be a place where you create value."* Aristotle is deliberately unfinished. The child fills in the blanks. The parent prints the PDF. The mentor persona is configurable, the kid could rename the tutor after their favorite philosopher tomorrow and the whole thing bends to it. Nothing is automated for them. Everything is designed with them.

## Why this particular design

**Weekly, not daily.** Daily makes practice a chore. Weekly makes it an event. A PDF that lands in the child's inbox every Tuesday at noon is something they can look forward to, and something small enough they can finish.

**Printable, not a web app.** A printed worksheet on paper is a deliberate break from screens. The child writes by hand, which the research on memory consolidation overwhelmingly supports. The parent can sit next to them without a device between them.

**Socratic, not explanatory.** Aristotle asks first, tells second. The scenario comes before the formula. The question comes before the answer. *"The person who asks the right question is always smarter than the person who memorizes the right answer."*

**Written by an AI to one specific child.** Generic worksheets are a relic of the factory. Aristotle reads your child's `CHILD_PROFILE.md`, their interests, their reading level, their cognitive style, and every scenario is written to that child. A kid obsessed with Percy Jackson gets demigod supply problems. A kid obsessed with Roman history gets legion quartermaster problems. The math is the same; the story is never the same twice.

**One illustration per exercise, not per section.** We started with one scene-setter per section. It was pretty but it faded from the kid's mind by problem 3. Moving to one scene per exercise, a different centurion, a different jarl, a different Spartan for every problem, was the single biggest engagement jump we measured. Kids stare at the picture for thirty seconds before reading the problem. By the time they start computing, they're already inside the story.

**Parent-in-the-loop by default.** The answer key goes to the parent. Aristotle isn't trying to replace the parent, it's trying to make the parent a better tutor by handing them the right questions to ask at the kitchen table.

---

## What this is not

- Not a homework-finishing bot. Aristotle generates *practice*, not submissions.
- Not a replacement for school. The child still goes to class, still takes tests, still has teachers. Aristotle fills the 30:1 gap: one child, one tutor, once a week.
- Not a SaaS. Everything runs locally on your laptop. Nothing your kid produces leaves your machine unless you explicitly email it to yourself.
- Not a business. MIT-licensed, free forever, built in the open so other parents can fork it and adapt it.
